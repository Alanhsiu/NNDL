Question:¶

In the cell below, summarize the findings of this experiment, and WHY these results make sense.

Answer:¶

Findings: The experiment clearly shows that the baseline model (without batch normalization) is highly sensitive to the initial weight scale. It only learns successfully within a very narrow range of initialization scales. If the initial weights are too small or too large, the baseline model fails completely, resulting in 10% accuracy (random guessing) and high loss. In contrast, the model with batch normalization maintains stable, high accuracy and low training loss across a significantly wider range of initialization scales (from $10^{-4}$ to $10^{0}$).

Why these results make sense: In a deep neural network without batch normalization, poor weight initialization quickly leads to issues. If weights are too small, activations shrink towards zero in deeper layers, causing vanishing gradients. If weights are too large, activations grow exponentially, leading to exploding gradients (which eventually causes numeric overflow or NaNs). Both scenarios prevent the network from learning.

Batch normalization solves this by explicitly normalizing the activations at each layer to have a zero mean and unit variance. This decoupling of the network's dynamics from the initial weight scale ensures that the gradients flow smoothly backwards, preventing them from vanishing or exploding. Therefore, it makes the model incredibly robust to suboptimal weight initializations and significantly eases the optimization process.
